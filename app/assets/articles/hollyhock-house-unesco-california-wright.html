When you think of Los Angeles’s biggest tourist attractions, or even its architectural landmarks, you might not think of Hollyhock House.

Commissioned by the oil heiress Aline Barnsdall and completed in 1921, the house was designed by Frank Lloyd Wright. But historically, it hasn’t drawn the huge flow of design-loving pilgrims that many of the architect’s most famous buildings do.

On sunny days, locals sprawl on the lawn surrounding Hollyhock House at what is now known as Barnsdall Art Park, picnicking or taking selfies with panoramic views. Getting inside the home for a self-guided tour, however, has typically required less planning than brunch.

That’s probably set to change, though: Last week, Hollyhock House became a Unesco World Heritage Site — part of an inscription that recognized eight of Wright’s major works.

He’s the first modern American architect to earn the honor, and, taken together, the group of works, which includes the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum in New York and Fallingwater in Pennsylvania, is one of just 24 Unesco World Heritage sites in the U.S. (You can explore the whole World Heritage List here.)

All of which means that Hollyhock House is likely to get a lot more visitors.

Officials who manage the property for the city of L.A., which owns the park and the house, told me they’re excited for the challenge.

“I think we’re definitely going to have to change our pitch from being a hidden gem to celebrating the fact that we’re a Los Angeles icon,” said Abbey Brach, the house’s associate curator.

She said she and her team had long drawn on the network of people who manage Frank Lloyd Wright houses around the country.

Dealing with larger crowds is old hat for, say, the staff at Fallingwater, which draws about 160,000 visitors per year. That’s compared with the roughly 30,000 visitors Hollyhock House hosts each year, according to Ms. Brach.

She said the Unesco recognition did not come with money, but it was a great boost for fund-raising to begin new digital initiatives and pay for restoration projects.

Christopher Hawthorne — a longtime architecture critic for The Los Angeles Times who recently became the city’s chief design officer — told me that as a part of Wright’s oeuvre, Hollyhock House is more than deserving of any new attention.

“It’s really a pivotal moment in his career — a final break from the Prairie Style houses he had become known for,” Mr. Hawthorne said.

The house’s design incorporates a range of architectural traditions, including Japanese and Mayan, in what Mr. Hawthorne described as a “radical hybrid” that influenced architecture in L.A. throughout the 1920s. He said the central Los Angeles public library is a great example.

Still, he said, it’ll be crucial to balance protecting the house, which is more fragile than a civic building, with accommodating more visitors.

And if you can’t get to Hollyhock House — or you’re hungry to see more of Wright’s California work — Mr. Hawthorne recommended the Marin Civic Center.
