Here’s a closer look at the performance of the senator by Jennifer Medina, who is covering the 2020 campaign:

During her first presidential debate, Senator Kamala Harris was on offense. Last night, now one of the Democratic field’s front-runners, she was forced to take on a defensive stance.

Following a breakthrough performance in last month’s Democratic debate, Ms. Harris has emerged as one of the party’s most closely watched candidates. She and former Vice President Joseph R. Biden Jr. in particular are seen as chief rivals after a highly personal dispute over segregation and school busing in that debate.

The visibility that followed gave Ms. Harris a significant boost in fund-raising and elevated her standing in polls of the primary field. The exchange also stirred many questions about Mr. Biden’s legislative history on issues concerning racial equality.

And so fireworks in the rematch seemed inevitable.

As Ms. Harris took to the stage Wednesday, Mr. Biden quipped, “Go easy on me, kid.”

Mr. Biden went after Ms. Harris by pointing out that San Francisco and Los Angeles have two of the most segregated school systems in the country. He also accused Ms. Harris of using “double-talk” on health care proposals, to which Ms. Harris retorted: “They’re probably confused because they’ve not read it.”

But the most searing critique of Ms. Harris’s time as attorney general came from Representative Tulsi Gabbard.

“She put over 1,500 people in jail for marijuana violations and then laughed about it when she was asked if she ever smoked marijuana,” Ms. Gabbard said of Ms. Harris. Then she went further, saying Ms. Harris owed an apology to “people who suffered under your reign as prosecutor.”

After the debate ended, Ms. Harris sought to dismiss the criticism, spinning it instead into a strength.

“Obviously I’m a top-tier candidate and so I did expect that I’d be on the stage and take some hits tonight,” she said in an interview with Anderson Cooper. (She spoke more than any other candidate except Mr. Biden.)

Tamra Fisher smelled smoke that morning. But that wasn’t so uncommon in Paradise. Small wildfires erupted in the canyons on either side of town every year.

“I’ve been here so long, it didn’t even faze me,” Ms. Fisher said, as she recalled the day the deadliest fire in California’s history ripped through her town.

Ms. Fisher would soon become one of thousands of people who were trapped in their cars trying to escape the area, each sealed in his or her own saga of agony, terror, courage or despair. She and others kept trying to scurry away from the fire only to discover that the blaze was ahead of them, behind them, alongside them, all around.

Within two hours of the first spot fires being reported near Ms. Fisher’s house, others leapfrogged from one end of Paradise to the other. It was unintelligible. As one man said, “I thought that the only part of Paradise that was on fire was the part of Paradise we were looking at.”

As Ms. Fisher tried to escape, one thing after another went wrong. She couldn’t reach her sister. She was trapped behind a wall of cars. Then something started beeping: the low-fuel alert. She was almost out of gas, though it ultimately wouldn’t matter. Moments later, her car caught fire.
