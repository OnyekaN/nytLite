LONDON — Residents of a small town in northern England have been ordered to evacuate after a dam on a 19th-century reservoir that looms above their homes was seriously damaged by heavy downpours.

Days after parts of Britain experienced record-shattering high temperatures amid a heat wave across Europe, the dam was damaged as a result of another type of extreme weather: intense rainfall that battered much of northern England and left the reservoir overflowing.

It could be days before 1,500 residents who left their homes on Thursday in the town of Whaley Bridge — population 6,500 — are able to return. Officials warned that the dam, which holds 1.3 million tons of water, could crumble and that the likelihood of “severe flooding” could put many in “mortal danger.”

On Friday, a Royal Air Force Chinook helicopter that had joined efforts to stop the dam from collapsing dumped more than 200 bags of gravel onto the damaged site, as engineers using dozens of pumps diverted water from the reservoir into the surrounding area, police officials said.

The government issued an alert on the potential for life-threatening flood in large stretches of the town.

The Toddbrook reservoir, which was built in 1831, sits high above the town. Managed by the Canal and River Trust, it is a Site of Special Scientific Interest, meaning that it provides habitat for herons, ducks and other animals and fish.

Whaley Bridge is in a valley, and some houses are above the reservoir water line. But flood experts fear that at least hundreds of properties could be at direct risk if the dam burst. The danger would be not only to the town below in the Derbyshire area, but also to many other villages along the Goyt River.

Martin Thomas, a local official, called the crisis “an unfortunate combination of circumstances.”

“We’ve had a lot of wet weather recently: The ground is saturated; the reservoir was already full,” he told the BBC. “Something we previously looked at as a benign leisure facility suddenly becomes a real mortal danger.”

Images and video online showed rushing, yellowish waters in the area after heavy rains brought flash floods that damaged roadways, shut rail lines and flooded homes and businesses.

Kem Mehmet, the assistant chief constable of the Derbyshire Police, urged the town’s residents to heed emergency warnings and advised those told to evacuate to stay away from their homes.

“We understand that being asked to leave your home is an extremely difficult and worrying situation to find yourself in,” Mr. Mehmet said in a statement. “However it is not a decision we have taken lightly, and ultimately the safety of the public is our main concern.”

Pete Fotheringham, a resident of Whaley Bridge, said he had evacuated above the level of the dam and was staying with friends, but he described a “lovely morning” in the village since rains had abated.

Neighboring communities offered shelter and support for the displaced, while charities and aid groups mobilized to offer supplies for those in need.

It was unclear how long the operation to stabilize the dam would take. Mr. Mehmet, the police official, said emergency services crews were “doing everything humanly possible to save the reservoir wall and to protect the town.”

A spokesman for the trust said by email on Friday that the dam was inspected twice weekly. It was last inspected on Thursday, and “this identified the initial failure.”

Crews are “working to draw down the water within the reservoir in a controlled way to reduce the risk to surrounding communities,” he said.

He said the residents of Whaley Bridge “have our utmost sympathy.”

“We are doing all we can, with our colleagues at the Environment Agency, Derbyshire Police, Derbyshire County Council and the Fire & Rescue Service, and further updates will follow,” he added.

Cleanup continued in other parts of the region, and crews were assessing the damage in Yorkshire, where a bridge over Grinton Moor was swept away by floodwaters.

The bridge is typically part of the Yorkshire World Championships cycling course, and officials said they would do their best to have it back up before the race in late September.

Boris Johnson, Britain’s new prime minister, said on Friday that emergency workers, engineers and the military were “working around the clock to fix the dam.”

“My thoughts are with those who have had to leave their homes and all of those who are affected in #WhaleyBridge,” he said on Twitter.
